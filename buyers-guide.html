Buyer's Guide

A simple guide to everything you need to know when buying property in Uganda — from setting a budget to getting your title deed.

Buying property in Uganda is a significant investment. Whether you're a first-time buyer, a returning diaspora client or an experienced investor, understanding the process protects you and helps you move confidently at every stage.

Take a look at our guide below — and reach out to our team any time a question comes up.

Set a realistic budget before you start viewing. Account not just for the asking price, but for additional costs — stamp duty, legal fees, agent commissions and any renovation needed. Having your finances clear upfront means you can move quickly when you find the right property.

Work with a registered real estate broker who knows the local market and can represent your interests throughout the process. Your broker should be able to identify suitable properties, arrange viewings and guide you through negotiations without pressuring you toward a quick decision.

Verify the property's title status before making an offer. In Uganda, this means confirming the title at the Ministry of Lands, checking for any caveats, mortgages or encumbrances registered against the property, and verifying that the seller has legal authority to sell.

Conduct a physical site visit and inspection. Go to the property yourself and ask as many questions as you want — about the boundaries, the neighbourhood, the landlord's history with the property, access to utilities, and anything that may affect your decision.

Make your offer through your broker and negotiate the price and terms. Once agreed, both parties should sign a Sale Agreement — a legally binding document covering the full terms of the transaction, including the agreed price, payment schedule and any conditions.

Engage a lawyer to review all documents, conduct due diligence and handle the transfer process. Do not sign a Sale Agreement or pay any money without legal review of the documents first.

Complete the transfer of ownership at the Ministry of Lands, Housing and Urban Development. Pay stamp duty (currently 1.5% of property value for individuals), and ensure the new title is issued in your name before finalizing payment to the seller.

Once the title is in your name, collect all original documents — the certificate of title, sale agreement, receipts, and any other transaction records — and store them securely. If you're not based in Uganda, consider a trusted property management partner to maintain the asset going forward.
